Ideas Worth Testing

Offensive Cohort Test — Surface Why Top Converters Converted

Identify the top-performing 10% of conversion cohort from last 90 days. Interview or survey them on what convinced them at the moment of decision. Build a variant page that leads with the exact language, proof, and framing they described. Test against control.

Hypothesis: Most pages are built around what the team thinks convinced visitors. If the actual conviction driver is different (e.g., a specific customer story, a particular outcome claim, a social proof element buried below fold), surfacing it above the fold will increase conversion rate. This is offensive testing: expanding a win rather than fixing a drop.

UGC Above-the-Fold Replace Hero Product Shot

Replace the hero section's product screenshot or lifestyle image with a collage of real customer use cases: actual screenshots of customers' work output, short captions of specific outcomes. Measure scroll depth, time-on-page, and trial/signup initiation rate.

Hypothesis: Following Talia Wolf's UGC conversion data, showing real users' real workflows in the first scroll will reduce hesitation and increase sign-up rate, particularly for SaaS where the product is abstract. Self-image and social-image triggers activate when visitors see people like them succeeding.

Traffic Source Intent Segmentation — Messaging Variant by Referral Type

Segment incoming traffic by referral type: AI search referrals (Perplexity, ChatGPT, etc.) vs. organic search vs. social vs. direct. Serve a variant to AI-referred traffic that leads with Level 0 clarity (what it is, who it's for, what problem it solves) before any feature or benefit claim. Serve the standard page to organic search.

Hypothesis: AI-referred visitors arrive with partial but unverified intent. They need to confirm a fit hypothesis quickly before engaging with value props. Leading with categorical clarity for this segment will reduce bounce rate and increase time-on-page. Search visitors who self-selected via keyword can be served the conviction-first version.

Activation Moment Surfacing — In-Product Test to Reduce Time-to-Value

Identify the single behavior most correlated with long-term retention and free-to-paid conversion. Build an onboarding variant that makes this specific action the first and only step visible on signup. Remove all other navigation and prompts. Measure activation rate, 7-day retention, and free-to-paid conversion.

Hypothesis: Per Verna's activation-first framework, onboarding flows that try to show everything delay the real 'aha' moment. A stripped-down, single-action onboarding focused on the highest-correlation behavior will increase activation rate and downstream conversion, even if early engagement breadth appears lower.
