Section 8

Ideas Worth Testing

Five-Component Messaging Audit → Sequential Copy Tests

Instead of running a single copy A/B test, run five sequential tests — each isolating one component (clarity / relevance / value / differentiation / friction). Identify which dimension is lowest-scoring first via qualitative panel or session review, then test specifically against that gap.

Hypothesis: targeted single-component tests produce larger lifts than omnibus copy rewrites, because they fix the actual problem rather than guessing at it.

Zero-Click Pre-Landing Impression Test

Test whether optimizing the meta description / search snippet / social preview card changes both CTR and post-landing conversion rate. Align snippet messaging with page hero — same emotional hook, same promise, no gap between pre-click expectation and on-page delivery.

Hypothesis: eliminating the expectation gap reduces bounce and increases scroll depth more than any on-page variant.

Journey-Level vs. Page-Level Optimization — Parallel Tracks

Run two parallel tracks: (A) optimize the landing page in isolation, (B) optimize the full journey from first ad/email touch to activation. Measure not just signup rate but activation-to-paid conversion 14 days later.

Hypothesis: journey-level optimization produces higher LTV impact per test than page-level optimization, even if the signup rate lift is smaller.

Your view: Experimentation-first, data-informed

At what product velocity does A/B testing become a liability?

Elena Verna argues that for fast-moving AI products, shipping velocity beats optimization cadence. PMF is a moving target. Running tests on a product that changes monthly means you're optimizing local maxima.

The real challenge: your experimentation system may be optimized for stable products with known funnels. If the product changes faster than the test cycle, what does responsible experimentation look like? The answer isn't to stop testing — it's to test at a higher altitude: the assumption layer, not the execution layer.
Your view: Analytics-first for hypothesis generation

Qualitative research may be the higher-leverage input for test ideation

Both Peep Laja and Talia Wolf argue that most test hypotheses are wrong from the start — because teams identify where people drop off (quant) without understanding why (qual).

If your test ideation pipeline is driven primarily by heatmaps, funnels, and session recordings, you're identifying friction points without understanding the emotional or messaging gap underneath them. The result: tests that optimize mechanics, not resonance — and those tests tend to produce lower impact per experiment.
